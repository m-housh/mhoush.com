# We're doomed, and it's not AI

A few years ago I wrote an article discussing
[heat recovery chillers](https://mhoush.com/articles/2023/heat-recovery-chiller/) and hydronic
HVAC systems. At the time, it was mostly a thought exercise around what I felt was an elegant
and flexible way to approach heating and cooling. Looking back now, I think the more important
discussion was not the specific equipment itself, but the direction the industry may eventually
need to move toward.

I still believe there are a lot of advantages to hydronic based systems and thermal storage,
especially as we continue moving toward electrification and lower GWP refrigerants. But this
article is less about promoting some dream mechanical room and more about discussing something
I think the HVAC industry needs to start taking seriously now:

We cannot wait until major market pressures fully arrive before we begin building the knowledge
and experience required to support architectural changes in HVAC systems.

Equipment can change fast. Training people and building experience takes years.

## This Was Never About Being "Anti Air"

I want to make this clear upfront because people tend to immediately jump into camps anytime
hydronics get discussed.

I do not think moving air is bad.

Air movement is critical for:

- ventilation,
- filtration,
- humidity control,
- indoor air quality,
- air mixing,
- and overall comfort.

The issue is that in the U.S. we tend to ask one system to do everything:

- heating,
- cooling,
- humidity management,
- filtration,
- fresh air,
- zoning,
- air mixing,
- and sometimes even solve building envelope problems.

> We have become very good at moving air. The question is whether air should continue being the
> primary transport method for all thermal energy inside buildings.
>
> Those are not the same thing.

## Refrigerants Were Always Part Of The Point

When I originally wrote about heat recovery chillers, part of my frustration was that we seemed
to be heading toward another round of transitional refrigerants instead of asking a harder
question:

If we are likely going to end up with natural, lower-GWP, and possibly flammable refrigerants
anyway, why are we not designing systems around that future now?

That is one reason the hydronic chiller concept still makes sense to me.

A self-contained outdoor hydronic unit potentially changes several things:

- refrigerant charge stays outside,
- less refrigerant is distributed throughout the structure,
- water becomes the transport medium indoors,
- thermal storage becomes easier,
- and future refrigerant transitions may become easier to adapt to.

## The Industry Already Knows How To Do This

One thing I think gets lost in these conversations is that most of these concepts are not new.

The industry already understands:

- hydronics,
- chilled water,
- geothermal,
- fan coils,
- thermal storage,
- reheat dehumidification,
- central plants,
- simultaneous heating and cooling,
- low temperature distribution,
- and dedicated outdoor air systems.

None of this is science fiction. The challenge is packaging, simplifying, teaching,
commissioning, and scaling these ideas into a residential and light commercial market that is
already struggling with labor shortages and training gaps. That is a very different problem
than inventing new technology.

## We Should Have Started Earlier

This is probably the biggest point I want to make.

> If the industry believes the future involves:
>
> - electrification,
> - new refrigerants,
> - higher efficiency expectations,
> - more advanced controls,
> - better humidity management,
> - and reduced energy usage,
>
> then we should have started discussing transitional system architectures years ago.

Not because every home needs a hydronic system tomorrow, but because you do not create
experienced designers, installers, and service technicians overnight. If we wait until the
market is forced to change before we begin learning how to design, install, and service these
systems, we are already behind.

## The Real Problem Is Transition Paths

I think this is where a lot of discussions fall apart.

It is easy to say:

> "The industry should change."

It is much harder to propose realistic paths that manufacturers, contractors, distributors,
educators, and homeowners can actually adopt.

Manufacturers are dealing with:

- liability,
- regulations,
- training realities,
- supply chains,
- warranty risk,
- and the fact that HVAC systems still need to be installable by the average contractor.

I do not think the manufacturers are stupid. I also do not think the field is stupid.

In many cases the field sees the practical limitations first:

- humidity problems,
- oversized systems,
- difficult zoning,
- serviceability issues,
- comfort complaints,
- refrigerant concerns,
- and increasingly complex communicating systems.

At the same time, manufacturers are trying to build products that can survive at scale in the
real world, which I'm sure is not easy.

## We Need Transitional Systems

I do not think the answer is immediately jumping to fully custom mechanical rooms with advanced
controls that only a handful of people understand.

The industry probably needs transitional products first:

- packaged hydronic modules,
- simplified piping groups,
- integrated controls,
- self commissioning systems,
- improved instrumentation,
- easier diagnostics,
- and hybrid systems that still feel familiar to contractors.

The systems that succeed will probably not be the most elegant systems.

They will be the systems that:

- reduce risk,
- reduce callbacks,
- are easier to understand,
- easier to commission,
- and easier to support in the field.

## Instrumentation Matters More Than Ever

One thing I have become increasingly convinced of is that instrumentation and observability are
a huge part of the future.

More advanced systems become much less scary when they are:

- measurable,
- observable,
- diagnosable,
- and verifiable.

A lot of field frustration comes from systems operating as black boxes. As systems become more
complex, we need better tools and better visibility into what they are actually doing. Luckily
we've made huge strides in this area with `measureQuick` and all the bluetooth probes that have
become common. The pieces that are lacking, in general, would be for `remote` obervability.

## Final Thoughts

I still like the original heat recovery chiller concept. I still think hydronics and thermal
storage deserve more attention in the U.S. market. But honestly, I think the larger issue now
is industry readiness. The HVAC industry has become extremely good at replacing equipment. The
next challenge may be learning how to evolve architecture. And that process starts long before
the market is forced to change.
